Merkle trees and append-only logs

Suppose we publish a list — every identity key we've ever issued, say — and claim we'll only ever add to it, never quietly edit or delete. How would you catch us if we cheated?

Downloading the whole list every day and comparing doesn't scale, and it only works if you never miss a day. The real answer is a structure called a Merkle tree, and once you see it, it's hard to unsee.

Start with hashes: a fingerprint of any data, where changing one bit changes the fingerprint completely. Now build upward. Take your list of entries and hash each one. Take those hashes in pairs and hash each pair together, giving half as many. Pair those and hash again. Keep going until one hash is left.

That final hash is the root. On our artifacts page right now it looks like this — a single fingerprint of the entire binaries log:

root: 37945cb2f61ee43782259a3893336b8ba8b8679d3af1612742deeec75e46cc0c

Here's the property that makes it worth the trouble: the root depends on every single entry. Change one character in one entry, anywhere, and its hash changes; so does its parent's, all the way up. The root becomes completely different. There's no way to alter an entry and keep the root the same — that would require two different pieces of data with the same fingerprint, the exact thing hash functions are built to make impossible.

So the root is a fingerprint of the whole list, in 64 characters. That's the leverage: instead of downloading a list that grows forever, you compare one short root. If you know the correct root, any claimed version of the list either matches or doesn't — there's no partial agreement.

The honest limit

A Merkle tree doesn't stop us from writing something false. We could add a dishonest entry, and the tree would happily include it. What it removes is our ability to be dishonest quietly — to show you one list and someone else another, or to change our story later. That's a smaller claim than "we're honest", and a much better one, because you don't have to take our word for it.

Track D · Transparency · Topic 8

Signed tree heads, proofs, and equivocation

You have a root: 64 characters fingerprinting an entire list. Two questions follow.

"Is my entry actually in there?" — the inclusion proof. You could download every entry and rebuild the tree, but you don't need to. You only need the hashes of the siblings along the path from your entry up to the root. Hash your entry, combine it with the sibling you were given, hash that, combine with the next sibling, and keep climbing. If you arrive at the published root, your entry is in the tree. If you arrive at anything else, it isn't.

For a list of a thousand entries, that's about ten hashes. For a million, about twenty. The proof grows like the logarithm of the list — double the entries, add one step. That's why this scales to logs that never stop growing.

"Is this the same list as before, only longer?" — the consistency proof. This is the one that enforces "append-only". You saw a root last week; there's a new root today. A consistency proof is a small set of hashes demonstrating that the old tree is contained inside the new one — everything you saw before is still there, unchanged, and the difference is purely additions.

If we had edited an old entry, or removed one, no such proof could exist. We couldn't fake it any more than we could fake a matching root. So "append-only" stops being a policy we promise and becomes a property you verify.

So who says which root is the real one? We sign it. Periodically we publish a signed tree head — the root, the number of entries, a timestamp, and a signature over all of it. The signature is made with one specific Ed25519 key, and Pollis apps and the pollis-verify tool have that key's public half hard-coded:

pinned key: 175ebfef98fc6b20c67c4cba9d4a36a4f85f05afa4e31f707e7d7e3c02227148

That pinning matters more than it looks. If the key were simply fetched from our server, a malicious server could serve its own key alongside a self-consistent fake tree, and everything would "verify" beautifully. Because the key is compiled into the client, a substituted key is detected immediately — in our own code that's a hard alarm, never a warning.

And now the attack this is all really about: equivocation. A log that only ever grows, with signed heads and proofs, still leaves one move: show different people different logs. A clean, append-only, perfectly-signed log for auditors — and a second one, with an extra key in it, served only to you. Each is internally consistent. Each verifies. They just aren't the same log.

The defence isn't cryptographic — it's social, and it's the reason "publish it" beats "promise it". Any two parties who compare the heads they were served will disagree, and since each head is signed by us, the pair is undeniable, permanent proof of misbehaviour. Nobody has to trust anybody; they just have to compare. Two heads claiming the same entry count with different roots is precisely what our own tooling flags as equivocation, and our publishing job keeps an across-run tripwire looking for exactly that.

So the chain is: a root fingerprints the list → an inclusion proof puts your entry in it → a consistency proof stops us rewriting it → a signature under a pinned key says it's ours → and comparison between observers stops us keeping two sets of books.

The honest limit

None of this makes equivocation impossible — it makes it undeniable once someone compares. If literally nobody ever checks the head they were served against anyone else's, two sets of books can sit side by side forever. The cryptography guarantees that comparison would settle it instantly; it can't guarantee that the comparison happens. Today the routine comparison is our own publishing job re-auditing what's actually being served plus a tripwire that fails loudly if a head at a given size ever changes — and anyone running pollis-verify. More independent observers is strictly better, and that's a limitation we'd rather state than paper over.

Track D · Transparency · Topic 9

Pollis's three logs — and what each one prevents

Three separate append-only logs, one signing key, one static site at verify.pollis.com. Each is worth describing by the attack it closes rather than by what's in it.

Log 1 — account keys. Stops: us handing you the wrong person's key. Every identity key Pollis publishes for an account goes in here, in order, with a version number. To substitute a key, we'd have to publish it — permanently, in public, where the real owner's own device is checking. The rule this log enforces is that versions only ever move forward: we can't insert an older key retroactively or renumber history.

Log 2 — the MLS commit log. Stops: us quietly rewriting a group's history. A group advances through epochs, and every member must apply the same commits in the same order — exactly the sort of thing a malicious server could tamper with by reordering, dropping, or forking it. So every commit is logged, and the rule is that a conversation is a straight line: each epoch appears once, and two different commits can never claim the same epoch. A fork is rejected at write time.

Note what this log holds: sealed commits. Publishing them exposes the shape of a conversation, never its content — the server never had the keys, and neither does anyone reading the log.

Log 3 — binaries. Stops: us shipping you a custom backdoored app. Every released installer's hashes go here. Its rule is no silent re-issue: the same release tag and platform can't come to mean different bytes later. If we ever built a special version for one person, its fingerprint is either in a public log everyone can read — or conspicuously missing from it. Both are detectable.

Why three logs and not one big one. Because a signed head for one tree must never be usable as a head for another. If all three shared a signature format, an attacker holding a valid head for the commit log could try to present it as a head for the binaries log. So each tree signs under its own domain-separated context — the signature commits to which tree it belongs to:

pollis-verifiable-log:sth:v1  ·  …:sth:v1:account-keys  ·  …:sth:v1:binaries

A head from one tree fails verification against another, structurally. Different questions, different logs, non-interchangeable proofs. They share one signing key and one static site, which keeps the thing you have to trust down to a single pinned key.

The honest limit

None of these logs makes us honest. A dishonest entry can be written to any of them; what changes is that it becomes permanent, public, and comparable across every observer — undeniable rather than impossible. They also do nothing about bugs: a log proves what we published, not that what we published is correct. And there's an operational limit worth stating plainly — the logs are rebuilt and republished on a daily schedule, so a brand-new release or a just-published key can be genuinely absent for a while. "Not in the log yet" and "not in the log" are different statements, and the app deliberately shows the first as pending rather than an alarm.

Binary transparency — and what reproducibility we actually have

Every other guarantee on this page assumes the app running on your machine is the app whose source you can read. Nothing survives a backdoored client. So this is the one to be sharpest about.

Code signing — the thing your operating system checks before letting an app run — proves less than people assume. An Apple or Windows signature proves "the holder of this company's signing key produced these bytes". It says nothing about what the bytes do. A backdoored build, signed by us under duress or after a compromise, passes every check your OS makes.

So we publish the fingerprint of every release into a public log. The question stops being "did Pollis sign this?" and becomes "is this exact app one Pollis published to the world, or one made just for you?"

Each release lands in the log at three layers, because "the app" isn't one file. The payload layer is the compiled program and its resources before any signature — the layer an independent third party can rebuild from source and compare. The signed layer is what you actually downloaded, after notarization or Authenticode; signing deliberately embeds unique, non-reproducible data, so the signed file can't be byte-identical between builds — by construction, not by sloppiness. We log it separately, bound to its payload.

The exe layer — the main program as installed — exists for a specific, slightly embarrassing reason, and it's worth telling straight.

When we first shipped the in-app "Verify this build" button, it compared the running program's fingerprint against the payload layer. That comparison could never succeed off Linux. The payload is recorded as a fingerprint of an extracted directory tree or an installer file — and an app that's already installed has neither of those things anymore. It has itself. So every genuine, correctly signed macOS and Windows release reported: "this build is not one Pollis publicly attested" — the loudest alarm we have, firing on honest builds.

The fix was a third layer recording the fingerprint of the main program as installed, which is exactly the thing a running app can measure. Two things are worth taking from that. First, it's the kind of bug that only surfaces when you actually build the verification path and use it — an argument for shipping this sort of thing rather than describing it. Second, releases published before that layer existed genuinely have nothing for a running app to compare against, so the app reports "can't check" rather than "you've been tampered with". That distinction is the difference between a useful alarm and a false accusation.

What it buys you. Two people download the app. One gets the public build, whose fingerprint is in the log. One gets a special build made just for them — and either its fingerprint isn't in the log, where that person's own app notices, or we did log it, in which case it sits in public, permanently, next to the real release. There's no quiet option.

Reproducible builds — and where we actually are. A logged fingerprint proves we published these bytes. It doesn't prove those bytes came from the source code you can read. Closing that gap means reproducible builds: anyone compiles the source at a given tag and gets byte-identical output. That's hard, because ordinary builds embed timestamps, absolute paths, and file ordering that differ per machine. Our honest status:

Linux AppImage: reproducible, given the published build recipe and a documented residual list — an independent rebuilder workflow, runnable from a fork with no access to any Pollis secret, rebuilds it and asserts the hash matches the log. macOS and Windows: not yet. Best-effort. This is the largest open gap in our verification story and we're not going to imply otherwise. The signing layer: never reproducible, by construction, on any platform.

There's also a second, independent anchor: every release carries a SLSA provenance attestation and a cosign signature, recorded in a public log run by neither us nor GitHub-as-us. That proves which workflow built the bytes, using no Pollis-held key on the verification path. It answers a different question from reproducibility — provenance says who built it, reproducibility says whether it matches the source — and having both means a single compromised key doesn't take down the whole story.

The honest limit

On macOS and Windows you currently have "these are the bytes Pollis published, and this workflow built them" — not "these bytes match the source you read". Only the Linux AppImage closes that loop today. We're stating it here, in your path, rather than in a footnote, because the gap is real and a verification story you have to squint at isn't one. The signed layer will never close it anywhere: signatures embed unique data on purpose.

Track D · Transparency · Topic 11

pollis-verify — verify it yourself

Everything above explains what could be checked. This one has you actually check it, on your own machine, in about two minutes. There are two ways in: reading the files directly, and running the verifier.

The verification API is just files. There's no clever API. verify.pollis.com serves a directory of static JSON — no server-side logic, no database query, nothing that could behave differently depending on who's asking. That's deliberate: a server that computes an answer can compute a different answer for you than for someone else. A server handing out flat files it wrote in advance has a much harder time, and the daily publish job re-reads what's actually being served to confirm it matches what was signed.

/v1/<tree>/sth/latest.json is the current signed tree head; /v1/<tree>/entries.json is the log's entries; /verify/release/<tag> is a pre-computed report for one release. And /v1/<tree>/public_key.json is the key the heads are signed with — which you should not trust. It's served for convenience; the whole point (Topic 8) is that real verifiers compare it against a key they already have. If it disagrees, that alone is the alarm.

Fetch the current head yourself — this works in any terminal, on any OS:

curl -s https://verify.pollis.com/v1/binaries/sth/latest.json
Live, fetched by your browser just now:
tree_size…
root…
That's the same number the artifacts page shows, and the same root the animations above use. Two places, one value.

The verifier does the actual checking. Reading JSON tells you what we claim. pollis-verify is a small standalone program that checks the claims: it re-hashes entries, rebuilds the tree, verifies proofs against the signed head, checks the signature against a key compiled into the binary, and re-runs the same rules the log enforces on writes. Crucially it's the same verification code the app uses — not a reimplementation that could drift into agreeing with us by accident.

Four subcommands, four questions. Arguments are positional, in this order:

# is the whole log internally consistent and append-only?
pollis-verify remote  https://verify.pollis.com

# what keys have we published for this user, in what order?
pollis-verify account https://verify.pollis.com <user-id>

# is this conversation's commit chain a straight, unforked line?
pollis-verify group   https://verify.pollis.com <conversation-id>

# are this release's binaries in the log, with valid proofs?
pollis-verify release https://verify.pollis.com v1.5.3

Reading the artifacts page and the in-app Security page

You now have every concept needed to read our public dashboards. Here's the tour — this is the legend for the map.

On the website: the artifacts page. Latest releases is the current version of each thing we ship, with download links. (Fun aside: for a while this displayed vv1.5.2 — a duplicated prefix — and the same bug meant the release-proofs lookup was querying a tag that could never exist, so the card permanently reported the newest release as missing from the log. A cosmetic-looking typo was hiding a functional failure.)

Release proofs is the heart of it: for the current release tag, was it found in the binaries log, did the tree verify, and what's in it. You'll see a row per artifact with the fields from Topic 10 — platform, bundle, layer (payload/signed/exe), the payload hash, the artifact hash, and an inclusion tick. Read a tick as "a proof was verified", not "a box was checked". tree size and root are the signed tree head from Topic 8 — and the root is the same string you'd get from curl or from pollis-verify. If it says not in log yet, that's usually the daily rebuild (Topic 9), not an alarm.

Daily transparency self-audit shows the current signed head of all three trees (Topic 9), republished daily — the "we re-check what we actually served" surface, including the tripwire that would catch us serving two different histories over time. And the one key you trust is the pinned Ed25519 key, 175ebfef…7148: everything above reduces to that single string. Worth reading the honest note attached — the page compares the served key against this constant in your browser, which is a convenience, not a trust anchor. Real verification uses a key you obtained independently (Topic 11).

In the app: the Security page. This build gives you a version, a commit, and a verdict from the in-app check (Topic 10). There are four:

Build publicly verified — this exact binary's fingerprint is in the public log for this release. Build publication pending — the release isn't in the republished tree yet; normal right after a release. Verification unavailable — couldn't check: the log was unreachable, or this release predates the exe layer so there's nothing comparable. Not an accusation. Build not in public log — the loud one: the tag is published, a comparable fingerprint exists, and ours isn't among them.

The distinction between those last two is the whole difference between a useful alarm and crying wolf — "I don't know" versus "you've been tampered with" — and we got it wrong once (Topic 10).

Below that: account key, your own device re-checking that the identity key the world is shown for you is the key you actually hold (Topics 6 and 9) — silent when fine. Devices and security events, an append-only local record of enrollments, rejections, and identity resets; not cryptography, just an audit trail worth reading if something feels wrong. And safety numbers per contact, with a distinct warning for a pinned key that changed.

The honest limit

Green across every one of these surfaces means: the things Pollis published are consistent, permanent, and include the app you're running. It does not mean the code is free of bugs, that your device isn't compromised, or that metadata isn't visible to the people who carry your traffic. We've shrunk what you have to take on faith and published the evidence for what's left — which is the whole promise, and a smaller one than "trust us".
