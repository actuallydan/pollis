Merkle trees and append-only logs

Suppose we publish a list — every identity key we've ever issued, say — and claim we'll only ever add to it, never quietly edit or delete. How would you catch us if we cheated?

Downloading the whole list every day and comparing doesn't scale, and it only works if you never miss a day. The real answer is a structure called a Merkle tree, and once you see it, it's hard to unsee.

Start with hashes: a fingerprint of any data, where changing one bit changes the fingerprint completely. Now build upward. Take your list of entries and hash each one. Take those hashes in pairs and hash each pair together, giving half as many. Pair those and hash again. Keep going until one hash is left.

That final hash is the root. On our artifacts page right now it looks like this — a single fingerprint of the entire binaries log:

root: 37945cb2f61ee43782259a3893336b8ba8b8679d3af1612742deeec75e46cc0c

Here's the property that makes it worth the trouble: the root depends on every single entry. Change one character in one entry, anywhere, and its hash changes; so does its parent's, all the way up. The root becomes completely different. There's no way to alter an entry and keep the root the same — that would require two different pieces of data with the same fingerprint, the exact thing hash functions are built to make impossible.

So the root is a fingerprint of the whole list, in 64 characters. That's the leverage: instead of downloading a list that grows forever, you compare one short root. If you know the correct root, any claimed version of the list either matches or doesn't — there's no partial agreement.

The honest limit

A Merkle tree doesn't stop us from writing something false. We could add a dishonest entry, and the tree would happily include it. What it removes is our ability to be dishonest quietly — to show you one list and someone else another, or to change our story later. That's a smaller claim than "we're honest", and a much better one, because you don't have to take our word for it.

Signed tree heads, proofs, and equivocation

You have a root: 64 characters fingerprinting an entire list. Two questions follow.

"Is my entry actually in there?" — the inclusion proof. You could download every entry and rebuild the tree, but you don't need to. You only need the hashes of the siblings along the path from your entry up to the root. Hash your entry, combine it with the sibling you were given, hash that, combine with the next sibling, and keep climbing. If you arrive at the published root, your entry is in the tree. If you arrive at anything else, it isn't.

For a list of a thousand entries, that's about ten hashes. For a million, about twenty. The proof grows like the logarithm of the list — double the entries, add one step. That's why this scales to logs that never stop growing.

"Is this the same list as before, only longer?" — the consistency proof. This is the one that enforces "append-only". You saw a root last week; there's a new root today. A consistency proof is a small set of hashes demonstrating that the old tree is contained inside the new one — everything you saw before is still there, unchanged, and the difference is purely additions.

If we had edited an old entry, or removed one, no such proof could exist. We couldn't fake it any more than we could fake a matching root. So "append-only" stops being a policy we promise and becomes a property you verify.

So who says which root is the real one? We sign it. Periodically we publish a signed tree head — the root, the number of entries, a timestamp, and a signature over all of it. The signature is made with one specific Ed25519 key, and Pollis apps and the pollis-verify tool have that key's public half hard-coded:

pinned key: 175ebfef98fc6b20c67c4cba9d4a36a4f85f05afa4e31f707e7d7e3c02227148

That pinning matters more than it looks. If the key were simply fetched from our server, a malicious server could serve its own key alongside a self-consistent fake tree, and everything would "verify" beautifully. Because the key is compiled into the client, a substituted key is detected immediately — in our own code that's a hard alarm, never a warning.

And now the attack this is all really about: equivocation. A log that only ever grows, with signed heads and proofs, still leaves one move: show different people different logs. A clean, append-only, perfectly-signed log for auditors — and a second one, with an extra key in it, served only to you. Each is internally consistent. Each verifies. They just aren't the same log.

The defence isn't cryptographic — it's social, and it's the reason "publish it" beats "promise it". Any two parties who compare the heads they were served will disagree, and since each head is signed by us, the pair is undeniable, permanent proof of misbehaviour. Nobody has to trust anybody; they just have to compare. Two heads claiming the same entry count with different roots is precisely what our own tooling flags as equivocation, and our publishing job keeps an across-run tripwire looking for exactly that.

So the chain is: a root fingerprints the list → an inclusion proof puts your entry in it → a consistency proof stops us rewriting it → a signature under a pinned key says it's ours → and comparison between observers stops us keeping two sets of books.

The honest limit

None of this makes equivocation impossible — it makes it undeniable once someone compares. If literally nobody ever checks the head they were served against anyone else's, two sets of books can sit side by side forever. The cryptography guarantees that comparison would settle it instantly; it can't guarantee that the comparison happens. Today the routine comparison is our own publishing job re-auditing what's actually being served plus a tripwire that fails loudly if a head at a given size ever changes — and anyone running pollis-verify. More independent observers is strictly better, and that's a limitation we'd rather state than paper over.

Pollis's three logs — and what each one prevents

Three separate append-only logs, one signing key, one static site at verify.pollis.com. Each is worth describing by the attack it closes rather than by what's in it.

Log 1 — account keys. Stops: us handing you the wrong person's key. Every identity key Pollis publishes for an account goes in here, in order, with a version number. To substitute a key, we'd have to publish it — permanently, in public, where the real owner's own device is checking. The rule this log enforces is that versions only ever move forward: we can't insert an older key retroactively or renumber history.

Log 2 — the MLS commit log. Stops: us quietly rewriting a group's history. A group advances through epochs, and every member must apply the same commits in the same order — exactly the sort of thing a malicious server could tamper with by reordering, dropping, or forking it. So every commit is logged, and the rule is that a conversation is a straight line: each epoch appears once, and two different commits can never claim the same epoch. A fork is rejected at write time.

Note what this log holds: sealed commits. Publishing them exposes the shape of a conversation, never its content — the server never had the keys, and neither does anyone reading the log.

Log 3 — binaries. Stops: us shipping you a custom backdoored app. Every released installer's hashes go here. Its rule is no silent re-issue: the same release tag and platform can't come to mean different bytes later. If we ever built a special version for one person, its fingerprint is either in a public log everyone can read — or conspicuously missing from it. Both are detectable.

Why three logs and not one big one. Because a signed head for one tree must never be usable as a head for another. If all three shared a signature format, an attacker holding a valid head for the commit log could try to present it as a head for the binaries log. So each tree signs under its own domain-separated context — the signature commits to which tree it belongs to:

pollis-verifiable-log:sth:v1  ·  …:sth:v1:account-keys  ·  …:sth:v1:binaries

A head from one tree fails verification against another, structurally. Different questions, different logs, non-interchangeable proofs. They share one signing key and one static site, which keeps the thing you have to trust down to a single pinned key.

The honest limit

None of these logs makes us honest. A dishonest entry can be written to any of them; what changes is that it becomes permanent, public, and comparable across every observer — undeniable rather than impossible. They also do nothing about bugs: a log proves what we published, not that what we published is correct. And there's an operational limit worth stating plainly — the logs are rebuilt and republished on a daily schedule, so a brand-new release or a just-published key can be genuinely absent for a while. "Not in the log yet" and "not in the log" are different statements, and the app deliberately shows the first as pending rather than an alarm.
