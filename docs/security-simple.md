Pollis is an app for sending messages. You can use it to chat with one friend, with a small group, or in larger spaces with channels — a lot like the apps you might already use for talking with family, coworkers, or a club.

The thing that makes Pollis different is privacy. When you write a message, it has to travel from your computer to the other person's computer. On the way, it passes through machines that we — the people who built Pollis — own. With most messaging apps, the people running the app could, if they wanted to, read what you wrote as it passes through. We can't.

The simplest way to picture it is a sealed envelope, but a stronger one than the paper kind. Before your message leaves your computer, your computer puts it inside a kind of digital envelope that only the person you're writing to can open. The mail carrier — that's us — sees the addresses on the outside, and roughly how big the envelope is, but we have no way to open it. Unlike a paper envelope, which somebody could steam open if they tried hard enough, this one is sealed with math: only the person it's addressed to has the key, and without that key the envelope is genuinely impossible to read. Even if somebody broke into our offices and took everything we have, the messages inside the envelopes would still be unreadable.

Groups work the same way. When you're in a group, your message goes inside an envelope that only the people currently in the group can open. If somebody leaves the group, the envelopes change so that person can't open new messages anymore. If somebody new joins, they can read what's said from the moment they joined onward, but not what came before. That's on purpose. It's a trade-off we made to keep things safe.

To use Pollis, you sign in with your email. We send you a one-time code, and once you type it in, your computer remembers you. You also pick a four-digit PIN. The PIN is what unlocks the app on your computer. If somebody finds your laptop and tries to guess the PIN, they only get a few tries before the app refuses to open. After enough wrong guesses, the app erases your messages on that computer for good. This is to protect you if your computer is ever lost or stolen.

When you first sign up, you also get a long backup code — a string of letters and numbers. Save it somewhere safe. A password manager works. So does a piece of paper in a drawer. If you ever lose your computer, or forget your PIN, that code is the only way to get your account back. Without it, we can't help you, because we don't keep a copy of it. That's the price of us not being able to read your messages: we also can't open them for you if you get locked out.

If you want to use Pollis on more than one device — say, a laptop and a desktop — you can. You sign in on the new one, and the old one shows a code. You type that code into the new one, and the two devices agree privately to trust each other. We don't see how that agreement happens. New devices start empty: they don't get your old messages. From the moment a new device is set up, both devices will receive everything going forward, but the past stays on the device where it was first received.

Files you share — pictures, documents, voice notes — get sealed in the same way before they leave your computer, and we store them sealed. We can't open them either.

Voice chat works a little differently. When you join a voice channel and talk to people, your voice travels through our servers in a form we could technically listen to, even though we don't and we don't record it. It isn't sealed in the same complete way that your text messages and files are. We're not going to pretend otherwise. So while voice chat works and is reasonable for everyday conversation, anything you'd be uncomfortable with a stranger overhearing should probably not be said over voice.

A few honest trade-offs to know about. Because we can't read your messages, we can't get them back for you if you lose everything. If you lose all your devices and your backup code, your messages are gone. We can also see who you talk to and roughly when, even though we can't see what's said — that's just how the internet works under the hood. If somebody steals your computer while it's already unlocked and signed in, they can read your messages, because at that point the computer thinks they're you. And the privacy is only as strong as the people you're talking to: if your friend hands their phone to someone else, that someone can read what you sent.

What this means in plain terms: most chat apps you've used — Slack, Microsoft Teams, Discord — can read everything you send. They don't usually mention it, but their servers see it all. Pollis can't. Apps like Signal and WhatsApp also seal messages the way Pollis does, and they also seal voice calls. We seal messages and files. We don't yet seal voice the same way.

That's the whole picture. It works like a normal messaging app, except we built it so we couldn't snoop even if we wanted to or were forced to. The cost is that you have to remember your PIN and keep your backup code somewhere safe, because there's no help desk that can let you back in.
